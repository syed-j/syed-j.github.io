// Overview

Project Scope

Built a comprehensive Excel workbook analyzing how each of the 50 US states protects consumers on medical billing, debt collection, and healthcare access. Each state was scored on 20 binary/partial policies, from Medicaid expansion to surprise billing limits to wage garnishment restrictions, producing a composite "Total Policy Score." The workbook features VLOOKUP-powered state comparison tools, 3 pivot table analyses by region, and filtered views to isolate specific policy adoption patterns.

// Key findings

Insights & Outcomes

East Leads, South Lags
Eastern states averaged 6.6 policy score vs the South's 4.1, a 61% gap. Maryland alone (14.0) scored nearly 3x the national average of 5.2, driven by strong billing transparency and debt protection laws.
Medicaid Does Not Equal Full Protection
While 37 states expanded Medicaid, only 4 require payment plans before collections and just 1 prohibits selling medical debt, showing expansion alone doesn't ensure consumer protection.
Debt Collection Blind Spot
Only 2 states restrict bank account seizure for medical debt and 4 disclose their collection policies. The most common protections (Medicaid, arrest prohibition) leave financial enforcement largely unregulated.
12-Point Score Spread
The gap between Maryland (14) and Tennessee (2) reveals vastly different levels of healthcare consumer protection. Patients in the lowest-scoring states have minimal safeguards against aggressive medical billing.
// Excel techniques

Workbook Features

VLOOKUP Comparisons
Built a dynamic comparison tool: enter any two state codes and instantly see a side-by-side breakdown of all 20 policies with auto-populated names, regions, and scores.
3 Pivot Table Analyses
Average policy score by region, state-level adoption of key policies (Medicaid, surprise billing, itemized bills), and filtered score rankings for selected states.
Filtered Views
Three pre-built filter configurations for quick analysis: isolating states by region, by specific policy adoption, and by score thresholds.
